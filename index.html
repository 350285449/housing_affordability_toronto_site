Research frame

Less report, clearer argument.

The question stays focused: how does affordable housing availability affect homelessness in Toronto?

Research question

How does affordable housing availability impact homelessness rates in Toronto?

Housing is not the only cause of homelessness, but without affordable units and support services, people have fewer ways to leave shelters or avoid losing housing.

Stance

Increasing affordable and supportive housing should reduce homelessness.

The evidence points toward a practical social justice claim: stable housing improves health, school, work, safety, and family stability.

Dependent variable Homelessness rates
Key drivers Affordable units, rent levels, policy, income, mental health supports

Part B

Research with the long text tucked away.

City of Toronto 2024 Street Needs Assessment

The City estimated 15,418 people experiencing homelessness in 2024. The count supports the claim that homelessness is large and urgent, but point-in-time methods can miss hidden homelessness.

Statistics Canada Housing affordability measures

StatsCan's shelter-cost-to-income approach shows why renters are vulnerable when housing consumes too much income. The limitation is that national or older data can understate current Toronto pressure.

Homeless Hub Supportive housing research

Supportive housing combines stable homes with services. That matters because some people need health, addiction, income, or settlement support alongside housing.

Policy proposals

Two actions match the evidence.

A

Tax second and additional residential properties more heavily.

Use the revenue for affordable and supportive housing. This directly connects housing wealth to housing need.

B

Prioritize affordable developments over luxury projects.

Build near transit, schools, and services so low-income households can stay connected to opportunity.

Limitations

Homelessness is also affected by income, migration, mental health, addiction, inflation, and data gaps. Shelter counts miss some hidden homelessness.

Response

Those limits do not erase the central pattern: when housing is unaffordable, prevention and shelter exits become harder.

Conclusion

Stable housing is the base layer of a fair city.

Toronto needs more affordable units, stronger renter protection, supportive housing, and policies that reduce speculation.
