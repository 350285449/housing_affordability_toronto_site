Issue and stance

The shortage of affordable housing affects stability, health, school, and work.

My research question is: How does the availability of affordable housing impact homelessness rates in Toronto?

Brief Summary

Affordable housing in Toronto has become harder to access because rent, ownership costs, and living expenses have risen faster than many people's income. This affects students, working families, newcomers, low-income households, and people already experiencing homelessness.

I take the stance that increasing affordable and supportive housing will reduce homelessness and housing stress.

Why It Is Social Justice

Housing is connected to safety, food security, mental health, education, and employment. When housing is distributed mainly by wealth, people with less money or fewer supports face higher risk.

The issue is not only individual choice. The data shows patterns by income, identity, and access to services.

Dependent variable Homelessness rate

Measured using shelter occupancy, Street Needs Assessment counts, or homelessness estimates.

Independent variables Affordable units, rent, policy, supports, economy

These factors can change housing stability and shelter demand.

Math proof of unfairness

Fair access would not produce these gaps.

This section uses one-variable statistics, ratios, percentages, and a simple probability experiment to show why the issue is unfair.

77%

Homelessness estimate increase

Toronto's Street Needs Assessment estimated 8,715 people experiencing homelessness in 2018 and 15,418 in 2024.

(15,418 - 8,715) / 8,715 = 0.769

2.5x

Ownership cost pressure

RBC reported that Toronto ownership costs required 75.1% of median household income in Q3 2024.

75.1% / 30% affordability benchmark = 2.50

3x

Overrepresentation

The City reports Indigenous people are about 3% of Toronto's population but 9% of people experiencing homelessness.

9% / 3% = 3

Probability experiment

Draw 100 people from a fair system versus Toronto's homelessness data.

If representation matched the city population, about 3 out of 100 people in the homelessness count would be Indigenous. The reported share is about 9 out of 100. The extra 6 out of 100 shows an unfair concentration of harm.

This does not mean identity causes homelessness. It shows that systems such as housing, income, health care, and colonial history do not affect everyone equally.

Notice

Parents gave higher average support than students for every policy. Both groups rated housing affordability much lower than policy support.

Conclusion Supporting My Stance

Low affordability ratings support my claim that the community sees housing as a serious problem. Policy ratings were strongest for public funding and affordable developments, showing support for public action.

Limitation

The survey used convenience sampling, so it may not represent all students, parents, or Toronto residents.

Call for action

Change starts with policy, community pressure, and practical support.

The typical person may not be able to build housing alone, but they can help shift attention, resources, and political pressure toward housing as a right.

01

Share the math

Use the 77% increase, 3x overrepresentation ratio, and 30% affordability benchmark to explain why this is unfair.

02

Support affordable developments

Speak up for affordable and supportive housing near transit, schools, libraries, and community services.

03

Contact representatives

Ask city and provincial leaders to fund affordable housing, strengthen tenant protections, and reduce speculative ownership.

04

Support local services

Volunteer, donate, or advocate for shelters, food programs, legal clinics, and mental health supports.

My two policy proposals

  1. Tax second and additional residential properties more heavily, then use the revenue for affordable and supportive housing.
  2. Prioritize affordable housing developments over luxury housing, especially near transit, schools, and community services.

Balanced conclusion

The data supports action, but it has limits.

Opposition View

Some people may argue that homelessness is caused more by income, mental health, addiction, migration, or job loss than by housing supply alone. They may also worry that higher taxes on property owners could reduce rental supply.

My Response

Housing is not the only factor, but it is the base. Without affordable homes, people cannot exit shelters easily, renter protection is weaker, and support services have fewer stable places to connect people to.
