Who gets to stay in Toronto?

Housing affordability is not only a market statistic. It decides who can live near school, work, family, health care, transit, and community.

Social justice argument

Affordability is a rights issue because housing shapes life chances.

01

Income decides geography

When rent absorbs more than 30% of income, households lose choice. Schools, jobs, child care, medical care, and support networks become harder to reach.

02

Renters carry more risk

Toronto's Housing Data Book says almost a third of households face affordability challenges, and renters are more likely to be affected than owner households.

03

Inequality compounds

City reporting identifies low-income households, renters, Black residents, Indigenous residents, and other racialized groups as disproportionately harmed by severe housing unaffordability.

Timeline

From 2000 to 2026, affordability became a defining Toronto question.

2000

A long affordability story begins

The timeline starts at 2000 to show that the crisis is not a single bad year. It is a multi-decade pattern of rising demand, uneven supply, and unequal access to stable housing.

Neighbourhood affordability

Affordability pressure is city-wide, but it does not feel the same everywhere.

Selected area

Downtown Core

96/100 pressure index

Highest competition for small units, strongest access to jobs and transit, and the greatest risk that service workers are priced away from the core.

  • Deeply affordable rentals near transit
  • Protections against renoviction and displacement
  • More family-sized non-market homes

What justice requires

Toronto needs supply, protection, and public purpose at the same time.

Build non-market housing

Co-op, public, supportive, and non-profit rental homes keep affordability attached to people, not only to market cycles.

Preserve existing rentals

Protecting older purpose-built rentals can prevent displacement faster and often cheaper than replacing lost affordability later.

Legalize complete communities

More mid-rise, multiplex, and family-sized homes near transit can reduce exclusionary pressure when paired with affordability rules.

Measure who benefits

Targets should track affordability depth, location, accessibility, tenure security, and outcomes for equity-deserving groups.

Conclusion

Housing should be treated as civic infrastructure.

A fair city cannot depend on displacement as its pressure valve. Toronto's affordability crisis calls for homes people can actually afford, stronger renter protection, public land used for public benefit, and policy that names the unequal burden carried by renters, low-income households, newcomers, racialized communities, students, workers, seniors, and families.
