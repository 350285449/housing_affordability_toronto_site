Project overview

Research question and stance

Research question

How does the availability of affordable housing impact homelessness rates in Toronto?

This project studies whether making more affordable housing available can reduce homelessness and improve stability for people under pressure from rent, ownership costs, poverty, health needs, and weak housing supports.

Stance

Increasing the availability of affordable housing will significantly reduce homelessness rates.

Affordable housing alone will not solve every cause of homelessness, but it is a core condition for prevention, shelter exit, family stability, and long-term social justice.

Dependent variable

Homelessness rates in Toronto

Independent variables

  • Availability of affordable housing units
  • Government housing policies and funding
  • Average rental prices
  • Mental health and addiction support services
  • Local economic conditions

Part B

Research and assessing bias

City of Toronto

2024 Street Needs Assessment

Summary
The SNA estimates homelessness across shelters, outdoor locations, respite spaces, institutions, and other emergency settings.
Personal connection
As a Toronto student, I see housing pressure around school, transit, families, and people who have nowhere safe to stay.
Supports my stance
The City identifies lack of affordable housing as a major factor that prevents people from exiting homelessness.
Bias or limitation
Point-in-time counts can miss hidden homelessness, people temporarily staying with others, and some people outdoors.
Opposition view
Some may argue homelessness is driven more by mental health, addiction, or migration than housing supply alone.
Statistics Canada

Housing affordability data

Summary
Statistics Canada measures affordability using shelter costs and household income, often using the 30% threshold.
Personal connection
Students and parents can both understand the pressure of housing when it is shown as a share of income.
Supports my stance
Renter households are more likely than owners to spend unaffordable shares of income on shelter costs.
Bias or limitation
National data may hide Toronto-specific pressure and may lag behind current rent increases.
Opposition view
Critics may say affordability measures do not prove that more affordable housing directly reduces homelessness.
Homeless Hub

Supportive housing research

Summary
Canadian Observatory on Homelessness and Homeless Hub resources explain how supportive housing combines housing with services.
Personal connection
The issue is not abstract: people need stable homes plus supports for health, safety, school, and family life.
Supports my stance
Supportive housing research shows that housing stability improves when people have homes and wraparound support.
Bias or limitation
Advocacy-oriented research may emphasize intervention benefits and may rely on studies from different cities or countries.
Opposition view
Opponents may question cost, neighbourhood impacts, or whether supportive housing should be prioritized over other services.

Sampling method
Convenience sampling through an anonymous Google Form shared with students and parents in the HCI community.
Open survey form

Rationale

Students may focus more on future housing affordability, while parents may have direct experience with rent, mortgages, taxes, and family housing costs.

Hypothesis

Parents will rate housing as less affordable because they are more likely to pay rent, mortgages, taxes, or bills. Students will still support affordable housing policies but may be less aware of financial trade-offs.

Benefits

The method is fast, simple, and useful for a school research project.

Limitations

The sample may not represent the whole HCI community. People who care more about housing may be more likely to answer.

Interpretation

Parents show stronger and more consistent support for interventionist housing policies. Students still support affordable housing policies, but their responses show more variability, likely because many students have less direct experience paying rent, mortgages, taxes, and bills.

Policy proposals

Two actions connect the evidence to social justice.

A

Tax second and additional residential properties more heavily.

Use the revenue to fund affordable housing. This responds to speculation and connects private housing wealth to public housing need.

B

Prioritize affordable developments over luxury housing.

Focus especially near schools, transit, and community services so low-income households can live close to opportunity.

Parts B and C showed that speculation, high rents, lack of affordable supply, and weak supports are major causes of housing inequality. These proposals aim to reduce speculation while increasing homes people can actually afford.

Opposition and limitations

Balanced critical thinking strengthens the argument.

Opponents might argue

  • Homelessness may also be affected by migration, inflation, job loss, mental health, addiction, and population growth.
  • Taxing property owners could reduce investment or rental supply.
  • Shelter data does not include all homelessness.
  • Ownership affordability data does not fully represent renters.

Response

These limitations matter, but the overall evidence still shows that housing affordability is worsening and that affordable housing supply, renter protection, and supportive housing are necessary. Housing is not the only variable, but it is one of the most important.

Conclusion

Stable housing is the foundation for a fair city.

Housing affordability is a social justice issue because stable housing affects health, education, safety, family stability, and opportunity. Toronto needs more affordable housing, stronger renter protections, better support services, and policies that reduce speculation.
